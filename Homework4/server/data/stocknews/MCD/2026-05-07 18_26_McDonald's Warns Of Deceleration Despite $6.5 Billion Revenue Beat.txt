McDonald's (NYSE:MCD) is leaning harder into value, bigger burgers, and new drinks as it tries to keep diners engaged, but management is also warning that the second quarter could slow as consumer sentiment weakens. After delivering a strong start to the year, the company said it expects meaningful deceleration in the current quarter, citing higher gas prices tied to the war in Iran and a tougher comparison against a successful promotion from last year. CEO Chris Kempczinski told analysts that consumers are facing heightened anxiety, making affordability a more important part of McDonald's playbook. That could explain why the chain is pushing several items under $3, including the Sausage McMuffin, while also offering a $4 breakfast combo.

The strategy appears to be built around giving consumers both cheaper entry points and reasons to trade up. McDonald's is highlighting its recently launched beverage lineup and a coming assortment of Red Bull-infused energy drinks, while the Big Arch burger has given some diners a more premium option. In the US, larger orders helped support first-quarter sales, with Extra Value meals bringing in low-income consumers, according to Kempczinski. The Big Arch, described as a hearty burger, cost as much as $10 in some New York and Chicago restaurants and attracted consumers looking for a splurge. Kempczinski said McDonald's is in great shape on value and affordability, a position that could be useful as the operating environment gets more difficult.
For investors, the first quarter showed a business that is still finding ways to drive demand, even as management signals caution ahead. Revenue came in at $6.5 billion, ahead of the average analyst estimate, while adjusted earnings per share of $2.83 also topped expectations. Established-restaurant sales rose 3.8% in the first quarter, slightly below the average of Bloomberg-compiled estimates, but still represented a rebound from a year earlier, when consumer confidence wavered ahead of new tariffs by the Trump administration. McDonald's shares rose less than 1% in New York on Thursday morning after paring earlier gains, while the stock had fallen 7% year to date as of Wednesday's close. The contrast with Shake Shack (NYSE:SHAK) was sharp, as Shake Shack reported revenue below the average analyst estimate, blamed inclement weather and a weak consumer backdrop for softer sales, and saw its stock fall as much as 29% after higher beef costs and other expenses hit profits.
