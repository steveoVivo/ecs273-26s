With Delta Air Lines trading at US$70.86, many investors are asking the same question: is the stock still offering value after a strong run, or has the easy upside already been priced in?
Over the last week the stock returned 5.4%, with 6.1% over the past month, 2.6% year to date and 60.1% over the past year, alongside 113.6% over three years and 64.0% over five years. This naturally shifts how the market thinks about both its potential and its risks.
Recent headlines around Delta Air Lines have focused on industry capacity, competition and changing travel patterns. All of these factors can influence how investors think about the durability of these returns. Together, these themes help explain why the stock has been drawing fresh attention from both existing shareholders and new investors.

A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model estimates what a stock could be worth by projecting the company’s future cash flows and discounting them back to today’s value using a required rate of return.
For Delta Air Lines, the model used is a 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity approach. The latest twelve month free cash flow is about $3.1b. Analyst based and extrapolated projections used in the model show annual free cash flow figures such as $2.4b in 2026 and moving to around $4.9b by 2035, all expressed in $ and then discounted back to today using Simply Wall St’s assumptions.
Bringing all of those projected cash flows together, the DCF output suggests an estimated intrinsic value of about $106.59 per share. Against the current share price of $70.86, this implies a 33.5% discount, which indicates that on this cash flow based view Delta Air Lines stock screens as undervalued.

For a profitable company like Delta Air Lines, the P/E ratio is a useful way to relate what you pay for the stock to the earnings it currently generates. Investors typically accept a higher or lower P/E depending on what they expect for future earnings growth and how much risk they see in those earnings.
Delta Air Lines currently trades on a P/E of 10.34x. That sits above the Airlines industry average of 8.48x, but below the broader peer group average of 19.88x. To give more context, Simply Wall St also uses a proprietary “Fair Ratio,” which estimates what a reasonable P/E might be for this specific company based on factors such as its earnings growth profile, industry, profit margins, market capitalization and risk characteristics.
This Fair Ratio is designed to be more tailored than a simple comparison with peers or the industry because it attempts to link the multiple directly to the company’s own fundamentals rather than just where others trade.
