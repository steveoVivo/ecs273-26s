The yen's sudden jump is putting Japan's currency defense back under the market spotlight, after a sharp move during Asia trading sent the currency to a 10-week high and fueled fresh speculation that authorities may have stepped in again. The currency surged about 1.8% in roughly half an hour, rising past 155.04 per dollar before giving back part of the move and trading around 156 as of 9:34 a.m. in London. The move came while Japanese markets were shut for a holiday on Wednesday, though analysts said the rally did not appear to be the result of thin liquidity. Japan's Finance Ministry did not immediately respond to a request for comment outside regular working hours.

For investors, the pressure point is still sitting near 160, a level many traders have been watching as a possible line where currency officials could act. Rodrigo Catril, a strategist at National Australia Bank, said the move has all the marks of intervention, adding that recent price action supports the view that Japan's Finance Ministry is trying to prevent the yen from moving toward 160 while also discouraging speculators from betting against the currency. The latest move follows late-April intervention by the Japanese government, its first since 2024, which sent the yen up as much as 3% intraday. Japanese officials have declined to directly confirm whether authorities stepped in, but people familiar with the matter said intervention took place on April 30, while analysis of Bank of Japan accounts suggests Japan likely spent around $34.5 billion. Goldman Sachs Group Inc. (NYSE:GS) analysts said Japan has the capacity to intervene 30 times at last week's scale, though officials are expected to conserve reserves and choose more effective moments to act.
That makes the yen a major macro signal for investors watching currencies, Asian risk sentiment, exporters and the broader path of global markets. Japan spent around $100 billion buying the yen several times in 2024 after the currency weakened to around 160.17, with additional steps taken when the yen reached 157.99, 161.76 and 159.45. Options pricing suggests traders are preparing for more action, with one-week risk reversals showing the most bullish yen sentiment since February and sitting near levels previously linked to intervention risk since 2022. Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama said Monday that speculative trading had been present in currency markets for a while, while top currency official Atsushi Mimura warned speculators last week with a final advisory if you want to escape and said the timing for taking bold steps is nearing. Jane Foley of Rabobank said the Finance Ministry's language last week was hard-hitting, and David Forrester of Credit Agricole CIB said reports about IMF guidelines may have emboldened investors to push USD/JPY higher, possibly giving the Finance Ministry and Bank of Japan another chance to defend around 157, which he said continues to look like the new line in the sand.
