The 30-year Treasury yield has moved back into the center of Wall Street's rate debate after briefly climbing above 5% this week, putting investors on alert for a possible shift in the long-term borrowing backdrop. That threshold matters because it helps set the US government's long-term funding costs and tends to stir concerns about bond vigilantes, tighter credit and higher rates lasting longer than markets previously expected. The latest move has been fueled in part by fears that the oil shock tied to the US war on Iran could push inflation higher again, with Brent crude jumping 40% since the conflict began and the 10-year US break-even rate rising almost 20 basis points. With the Strait of Hormuz still closed and oil around $100 a barrel, investors are now weighing whether central banks in the US, UK and Europe may need to hold a tighter line rather than move quickly toward rate cuts.

The bigger issue is that this bond-market pressure is landing on an economy that has stayed more resilient than many forecasters expected. US inflation-adjusted gross domestic product rose at an annualized 2% in the first quarter, while forecasters expect Friday's April jobs report to show a solid 62,000 increase in payrolls, faster wage growth and an unchanged unemployment rate. Even after the Federal Reserve began easing in late 2024, the 30-year yield has risen by nearly a full percentage point, marking the biggest increase during a Fed easing period since at least the 1980s. Traders who three weeks ago saw a 43% chance of a Fed rate cut by March next year now price in more than a 20% chance of a hike, reflecting how quickly oil, growth and inflation concerns have reshaped the rate outlook. The pressure is not limited to the US, as National Bank of Canada strategists estimate that average 10- and 30-year yields across Group of Seven countries ended April at a 17-year high, while BlackRock Investment Institute has said higher yields are likely here to stay.
For investors, the key question is whether this is another brief test of the 5% level or the beginning of a more durable repricing across bonds, equities and credit. US stocks have still reached new record highs as the AI spending boom and strong corporate earnings have outweighed Middle East risks and a slowing labor market, but Bank of America Corp. (NYSE:BAC) strategist Michael Hartnett warned that a sustained move in the 30-year yield above 5% is where the door to doom starts to open. History gives investors reason to watch the level closely, since previous sharp rises in long-term yields have been followed by recessions in 2001 and 2007, a 27% drawdown in the MSCI World Index in 2022 as yields moved quickly toward 4%, and an 11% decline over three months as yields approached 5% in 2023. A US-Iran deal, lower oil prices or a weaker economy could possibly bring rate-cut bets back into focus, but stronger growth, inflation pressure and US debt held by the public near $31 trillion suggest longer-dated Treasuries may remain a high-stakes trade rather than an easy safe-haven bet.
