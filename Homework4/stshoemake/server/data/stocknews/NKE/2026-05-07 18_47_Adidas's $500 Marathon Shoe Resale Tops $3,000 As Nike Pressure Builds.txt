Adidas (ADDYY) is starting to look like a much more direct threat to Nike's (NYSE:NKE) comeback story, after a powerful showing at the London Marathon gave investors a fresh signal that the German sportswear group is gaining ground in performance gear, not just fashion-led sneakers. Kenya's Sabastian Sawe and Ethiopia's Yomif Kejelcha both ran the men's race in fewer than two hours wearing Adidas's Adizero ultra-light racing shoes, while Ethiopia's Tigst Assefa won the women's race in the same footwear. Sawe set a new world record of 1 hour, 59 minutes and 30 seconds, Kejelcha finished just 11 seconds behind him, and Assefa broke the women's world record with a time of 2 hours, 15 minutes and 41 seconds. The key product behind all three performances was Adidas's Adizero Adios Pro Evo 3, a new 97-gram racing shoe that gave Chief Executive Officer Bjoern Gulden a high-profile performance win as he tries to turn Adidas's streetwear momentum into stronger credibility on the track and field.

For Nike, this could make an already difficult turnaround more complicated. The common investor narrative has been that Nike lost momentum partly because newer rivals such as On Holding (NYSE:ONON) and Brooks Running took share in running, but Adidas's London Marathon success suggests the pressure is also coming from a traditional heavyweight with brand scale, product innovation, and sharper fashion relevance. Adidas moved quickly after the race, putting up billboards featuring the shoe around the world within hours of the marathon's finish ahead of the $500 model's limited release last week. The shoe quickly sold out and was changing hands for more than $3,000, according to publicly available sales data from StockX. Nike responded with a more dignified campaign referencing Eliud Kipchoge, who ran a marathon in less than two hours in 2019 wearing Nike shoes, though that run was not eligible for world-record status. Nike still has signs of momentum in running, with sales in the category rising more than 20% year-on-year in each of the past three quarters, helped by its Vomero shoe, while the company has also introduced Nike Mind, neuroscience-based footwear designed to increase sensory input from the feet.
The next pressure point is the soccer World Cup, which begins on June 11 and could give both companies a major global stage to prove their performance and lifestyle strategies are still resonating. Nike has the home-market advantage because the tournament is taking place in its biggest market, and it is sponsoring the US and Canada teams while leaning on star players such as France's Kylian Mbappe and Portugal's Cristiano Ronaldo. Adidas is not giving up that field, with sponsorships of tournament favorite Spain, defending champion Argentina, and third co-host Mexico, while Gulden is also selling football-inspired streetwear such as Sambas in national team colors. Adidas has not escaped the broader slowdown in sports-inspired clothing and sneakers, with first-quarter sportswear sales rising 5% and its premium Originals line up 7%, but running products rose almost 30% and football boots and clothing were up almost 50%. For investors, the Adidas-Nike rivalry now looks less like a simple story of Nike battling smaller running upstarts and more like a deeper fight against a revived Adidas that could be using elite racing wins, football visibility, and streetwear strength to challenge Nike's return to former glory.
