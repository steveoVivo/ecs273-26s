Pfizer (NYSE:PFE) chief executive Albert Bourla has signaled a possible shift in tone around US vaccine policy, praising President Donald Trump's latest nominee to lead the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention after a turbulent year for the agency. In a Bloomberg TV interview Tuesday, Bourla called Erica Schwartz a very credible scientist, pointing to what could be a more conventional choice for the country's top public health role. Schwartz, who holds both medical and law degrees and previously served as deputy surgeon general during the first Trump administration, is now being viewed as a figure who could help stabilize an agency that has become increasingly important for vaccine makers. For investors, the CDC story matters because vaccine recommendations can shape demand, market confidence, and the operating backdrop for companies exposed to immunization policy.

The tension has been building under Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., whose approach upended childhood immunization policy and turned vaccines into a flash point for companies such as Pfizer and Moderna (NASDAQ:MRNA). A key federal vaccine panel was fired and replaced with several skeptics of Covid shots, which are manufactured by Pfizer and Moderna, and the panel later changed its recommendations for universal Covid vaccination. Kennedy then went further by moving beyond the committee and narrowing the broader childhood vaccine schedule. The leadership path at the CDC has also been unstable: Kennedy first selected former Florida Congressman Dave Weldon, but that nomination was pulled after it became apparent he did not have enough Senate support due to his past comments on vaccines. Susan Monarez was later nominated and approved by the Senate, but she led the agency for only 29 days before Kennedy fired her following a clash over vaccine policy, leaving the position to be filled by two acting directors since then.
Bourla's latest comments stand out because they mark a softer tone from earlier this year, when he openly criticized the administration's dismantling of long-standing vaccine recommendations during a January lunch with journalists in San Francisco. At the time, he said he was very annoyed, very disappointed, and seriously frustrated, arguing that the changes had zero scientific merit and served a political and antivaccine agenda. Kennedy had been a Covid vaccine skeptic during the pandemic and had often criticized Bourla on social media, while the administration also brought in other critics of the government's pandemic response, including Jay Bhattacharya to lead the National Institutes of Health. Now, Bourla appears more optimistic, saying the CDC had been a very painful story and that the new nominee is helping turn around the agency. With the Trump administration reportedly recognizing vaccines as a weak issue with voters ahead of the midterm elections, and Kennedy dialing down vaccine rhetoric after an internal memo encouraged officials to emphasize broader themes such as clean eating and medical fraud, Bourla said he is optimistic all these bad things that we saw with vaccines are in the past.
