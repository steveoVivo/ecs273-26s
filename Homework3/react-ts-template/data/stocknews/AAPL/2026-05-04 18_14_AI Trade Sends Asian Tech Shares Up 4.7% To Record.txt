Global equities rallied as investors returned to the AI trade, with Asian shares moving closer to all-time highs after stronger-than-expected earnings from megacap technology companies helped push Wall Street to fresh records. MSCI's Asian equities index rose 2.2%, putting it within reach of its Feb. 27 record, while gauges in South Korea and Taiwan both gained more than 4.5%. The strength was led by chipmakers, with Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. (NYSE:TSM) climbing 6.6% and SK Hynix Inc. (HXSCL) surging 11%. Futures tied to the S&P 500 (SPY) and Nasdaq 100 (NASDAQ:QQQ) also advanced after major tech earnings, including Apple Inc. (AAPL), supported new highs for US stocks on Friday. The rally suggests investors are still treating AI as the dominant market narrative, even as geopolitical pressure and elevated oil prices remain potential constraints.

The broader setup has become increasingly risk-on, with global equities rising for a month as traders largely looked past the economic fallout from Middle East hostilities. The S&P 500 posted its fifth straight week of gains, helped by signs of US economic strength, corporate resilience, and efforts to turn a fragile ceasefire into a more durable peace. Bloomberg-compiled data showed about 81% of S&P 500 companies had beaten first-quarter earnings estimates, while emerging-market equities reached a fresh record toward the end of April and Asian shares recovered their war-driven losses. Pepperstone Group strategist Dilin Wu said markets are performing well because of the AI-driven trade or AI-driven hype, but added that geopolitical upheaval and high oil prices remain a constraint. That leaves Asian equities possibly supported by bullish sentiment around AI, particularly in Korea and Japan, though the backdrop still carries meaningful macro and energy risk.
The more volatile signals came from oil and currencies. Brent crude initially fell 2.4%, then erased those losses and traded little changed near $108 a barrel after President Donald Trump said the US would begin guiding ships not involved in the Iran conflict through the Strait of Hormuz from Monday. A senior Iranian official said Tehran would view any US interference in the Strait as a ceasefire breach, according to AFP, keeping traders focused on whether smoother energy flows could actually emerge after a near-full blockade for two months. The yen briefly jumped as much as 0.8% against the dollar after last week's reported intervention by Japanese authorities, then pared the move to trade 0.2% stronger at 156.72, with Credit Agricole's David Forrester flagging the 157 level in USD/JPY as important to watch. Gold edged lower to around $4,600 an ounce, silver traded around $75.75, and Bitcoin rose 1% to about $79,800, while President Trump described talks with Tehran as very positive after Washington received Iran's latest proposal to end the war.
