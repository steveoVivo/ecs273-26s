If you are wondering whether NIKE's current share price reflects its true worth, you are not alone. The numbers tell an interesting story for valuation focused investors.
The stock last closed at US$44.40, with returns of 0.6% over the past week, 0.5% over the past month, and declines of 29.8% year to date, 22.3% over 1 year, 62.9% over 3 years, and 65.2% over 5 years.
Recent coverage has focused on NIKE's position as a leading global sportswear brand, with attention on how consumer demand, competition in athletic apparel, and brand strength may be influencing investor sentiment. These themes help frame why the share price has moved the way it has and why valuation is such a key question now.
Right now, NIKE scores 2 out of 6 on Simply Wall St's valuation checks. The sections that follow will walk through what that means using different valuation methods, while also pointing to a more complete way of thinking about value at the end of the article.

A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model estimates what a business might be worth by projecting its future cash flows and then discounting those back to today’s value. For NIKE, the model used is a 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity approach, based on cash flow projections rather than earnings or dividends.
NIKE’s latest twelve month Free Cash Flow is about US$1.04b. Analyst and extrapolated projections used in the model suggest Free Cash Flow reaching about US$3.91b by 2030, with a series of annual estimates between 2026 and 2035 that are discounted back to today using Simply Wall St’s assumptions. All of these projected figures are converted into today’s dollars to arrive at an estimated intrinsic value per share.
On this basis, the DCF model suggests a fair value of roughly US$38.50 per share, compared with the recent share price of US$44.40. That indicates NIKE is about 15.3% overvalued according to this particular cash flow based approach.
Result: OVERVALUED

For a profitable company like NIKE, the P/E ratio is a useful way to think about what you are paying for each dollar of earnings. Investors typically accept a higher P/E when they expect stronger growth or see lower risk, and look for a lower P/E when growth is slower or risks feel higher.
NIKE currently trades on a P/E of 29.22x. That is above the Luxury industry average of 19.54x, but below the peer group average of 33.71x. These simple comparisons indicate that the market is valuing NIKE’s earnings more highly than the broader industry, while not assigning it the highest multiples seen among peers.
Simply Wall St’s Fair Ratio for NIKE is 31.47x. This reflects its view of a reasonable P/E given factors such as earnings growth, industry, profit margins, market cap and risk profile. This Fair Ratio can be more informative than a straight comparison with peers or industry averages because it adjusts for NIKE’s specific characteristics instead of assuming all companies deserve the same multiple. Since the Fair Ratio of 31.47x is higher than the current 29.22x, NIKE’s P/E sits below this benchmark, which suggests that the shares may be undervalued on this metric.
